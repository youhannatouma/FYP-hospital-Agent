# 4. Analysis

## 4.1 Business Rules, Functional Requirements, and Non-Functional Requirements

The analysis of this project shows that the platform must be understood as a healthcare information system rather than a general-purpose digital application. Its structure is defined by the need to coordinate patients, doctors, and administrators through secure and traceable workflows that preserve the integrity of appointments, clinical documentation, and AI-assisted interactions. For that reason, the requirements of the system are best presented in three distinct categories. Business rules define the governing policies and operational boundaries of the platform. Functional requirements describe the services the system must provide to satisfy user and organizational needs. Non-functional requirements describe the quality attributes that ensure the platform remains dependable, secure, and suitable for healthcare use.

### 4.1.1 Business Rules

The first major business rule is the separation of user roles and responsibilities. The platform recognizes patients, doctors, and administrators as distinct actors, and each actor must operate within a clearly defined scope of authority. Patients are permitted to manage their own healthcare interactions, such as reviewing appointments, consulting their personal information, and interacting with the AI assistant for support. Doctors are responsible for managing clinical interactions, reviewing scheduled consultations, and producing medical documentation. Administrators are expected to supervise the broader operation of the platform, including governance and user oversight. This rule is essential because it ensures that the digital platform reflects the real organizational structure of healthcare service delivery and prevents unrestricted access to sensitive resources.

Another business rule concerns ownership and access control over personal and clinical data. The system must ensure that no user can interact with protected healthcare information unless that interaction is justified by their role and relationship to the resource. A patient must only access their own records, bookings, and care-related information. A doctor must act only within the professional boundaries assigned to them, especially when creating or consulting patient-related records. Administrative authority must remain limited to authorized supervisory actions rather than unrestricted clinical intervention. This rule exists to preserve confidentiality, accountability, and institutional trust, all of which are central to healthcare operations.

Appointment handling is also governed by strict business rules. A doctor’s time availability is represented through real bookable slots, and an appointment may only be confirmed if the chosen slot belongs to the intended doctor and is available at the moment of booking. Once confirmed, the slot must no longer be available for another reservation. If an appointment is cancelled, the corresponding slot should be reopened so that the scheduling system remains accurate and efficient. If an appointment is completed, its status must reflect that the consultation has taken place. These rules are necessary because appointment scheduling is not merely a convenience feature; it is a controlled operational process that directly affects clinical coordination and patient service delivery.

Medical records are subject to an equally important set of business constraints. Clinical documentation must originate from the doctor rather than the patient, since diagnoses, treatment plans, and consultation notes must be produced by a qualified healthcare professional. Each record must remain connected to the patient and the doctor involved, and where relevant it should also be linked to the appointment during which the clinical interaction occurred. This ensures continuity of care and preserves the context of medical decisions. In addition, the platform should avoid careless destruction of clinical data and instead maintain traceability in how records are managed. In a healthcare setting, the preservation of record history is not simply a technical preference but a business necessity tied to safety and accountability.

The role of the AI assistant is also constrained by a business rule that defines it as a support mechanism rather than a substitute for professional care. The assistant may guide users, answer questions, and help them navigate the services available on the platform, but it must not undermine formal clinical workflows or replace the authority of medical professionals. Its purpose is to improve accessibility and engagement while remaining consistent with the responsibilities already assigned to doctors, patients, and administrators. This rule is particularly important in healthcare, where digital convenience must always remain subordinate to clinical reliability and responsible decision-making.

### 4.1.2 Functional Requirements

From a functional perspective, the system must first provide secure user authentication and controlled access to protected services. Users must be able to sign in and have their identity validated before the platform allows them to perform sensitive operations. Once authenticated, the system must determine the role of the user and expose the appropriate resources and actions. This capability is fundamental because all other services, including appointments, medical records, and AI-assisted interactions, depend on knowing who the user is and what permissions they possess.

The platform must also provide comprehensive appointment management functionality. Patients should be able to view their appointments, book consultations with doctors, and request changes such as cancellation or rescheduling when permitted. Doctors should be able to view the appointments assigned to them and mark consultations as completed after the medical encounter has occurred. The system must therefore support the full appointment lifecycle from initial selection of an available slot through to final completion. This functionality is a core requirement because appointment coordination is one of the main operational purposes of the platform.

Another key functional requirement is medical-record management. Doctors must be able to create medical records that document diagnoses, treatments, consultation notes, and related encounter information. Patients must be able to access their own medical history so that they remain informed about their treatment journey. The system must also support retrieval of records according to user role and maintain an organized relationship between records, patients, doctors, and appointments. This function is central to the continuity of care because it transforms individual consultations into a coherent clinical history.

The project must also include an AI assistant that supports user interaction in a practical and integrated way. Patients should be able to ask health-related questions, obtain assistance related to symptoms or medications, and receive help navigating platform services such as finding a doctor or initiating a booking. The assistant should also preserve conversation flow within user sessions so that interactions remain coherent rather than fragmented. Functionally, this means the assistant is not an isolated chatbot but a connected service embedded within the broader healthcare workflow of the application.

In addition, the system must provide supporting administrative and informational capabilities that contribute to platform completeness. These include user oversight, structured retrieval of healthcare-related information, and the ability to maintain data that reflects the current operational state of the institution. While some of these functions may be less visible to the patient than appointment booking or record access, they remain necessary for ensuring that the system operates as a complete healthcare platform rather than a narrow single-purpose tool.

### 4.1.3 Non-Functional Requirements

The non-functional requirements of the system are especially significant because the project operates in a healthcare context where quality attributes are inseparable from practical usefulness. Security is one of the most important of these requirements. The platform must protect sensitive personal and medical information through authenticated access, role-sensitive authorization, and careful restriction of privileged actions. Security in this system is not an optional enhancement; it is a baseline expectation without which the platform would fail to meet the standards required for handling healthcare data.

Reliability is another essential non-functional requirement. The system must maintain consistent data states during critical operations such as appointment booking, cancellation, rescheduling, and record creation. If a process fails, it should do so safely without leaving the system in a partially updated or contradictory condition. This requirement is particularly important when handling bookable time slots and clinical records, where inconsistency could create scheduling conflicts or compromise the trustworthiness of patient information. For the same reason, the platform must include validation measures that ensure users, doctors, appointments, and records exist and satisfy expected conditions before operations are committed.

Usability also represents a major non-functional requirement. The platform is intended for use by individuals with very different goals and levels of technical familiarity, including patients seeking straightforward access to care and professionals managing operational tasks. The interface and workflow design must therefore remain clear, coherent, and supportive of efficient task completion. The AI assistant contributes to this requirement by reducing friction in navigation and helping users access services more naturally, but the overall system must still remain understandable even when users are performing sensitive or multi-step actions.

Maintainability is another important quality attribute for the project. The platform should be organized in a way that supports future development, extension, and correction without excessive complexity. A modular separation of concerns between authentication, scheduling, medical records, and AI-assisted features helps ensure that the system can evolve as new healthcare needs emerge. In a project of this nature, maintainability is not only beneficial for developers; it also supports long-term service continuity for the institution that relies on the platform.

Finally, the system must support accountability and traceability as non-functional requirements closely tied to the healthcare domain. Actions that affect appointments, records, and protected data should be attributable to authenticated users operating within recognized roles. The platform must therefore preserve a trustworthy relationship between identity, action, and stored outcome. This quality is essential for organizational confidence, for responsible system governance, and for ensuring that the digital platform remains aligned with the seriousness of the clinical environment in which it is used.
